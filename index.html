Report Structure
What goes in your 1,000 words?
📍 Context
80–100 words
Who, why, where, when, duration. Set the scene briefly — think journalist's lede.
🔧 Data Collection
120–150 words
How did you prep? What method? Semi-structured? Contextual inquiry? What tools — audio recording, Otter.ai?
📊 Data Analysis
120–150 words
Thematic analysis? Affinity mapping? Miro? Post-its? Show your thinking process.
💡 Findings & Insights
150–180 words
Their expertise, key skills, contradictions, surprises. What would IDEO call "unexpected gems"?
🔍 Reflection
500–550 words — The most important section. What worked? What didn't? How did you feel? What was unexpected? What will you do differently? This is where your designer's voice emerges.

Why does empathy
actually matter?
🏠 Airbnb · 2009
When struggling, founders flew to NY and photographed hosts' homes themselves. One conversation revealed guests wanted to "feel like locals." That insight became their entire brand.
🎧 Spotify · UX Research
Spent months interviewing commuters. Discovered that shuffle mode felt "unfair." The fix: a new algorithm that feels more random by being less random.
The most powerful design tool is listening.
"You can't do good design without understanding the people you're designing for."
— Tim Brown, IDEO CEO
This is exactly what you're practising in A1 — not just hearing words, but reading the space between them.

Interview Best Practices
The golden rules from the field
✓
Warmth, attentiveness, humour — put them at ease. You're a guest in their world.
✓
Zero judgement — no raised eyebrows, no "wow really?!" reactions that signal surprise.
✓
Never answer for them — if there's silence, sit with it. Silence is data.
✓
Active listening — "Tell me more about that…" / "Why is that?" / Repeat back their words.
✓
Use their language — avoid jargon. If they're a chef, say "mise en place," not "preparation systems."
✓
Don't be afraid of silence — count to 7 before filling the gap. They often continue.
✓
Follow the thread — pursue unexpected tangents. The best insights are off-script.
✓
Be present — phone away. Eye contact. Breathe. This is a human connection, not data extraction.

AI & Frontier Tech
Your AI
Usage Considerations
You CANNOT use GenAI for A1 to write the reports, but you can use it to assist your research and must acknowledge it. Even if you use Grammarly to check your grammar, you should acknowledge it in your Journal.
Using AI to generate report is dangerouis as Turnitin will detect it for failing the unit.
🎙️ Transcription
Otter.ai / Whisper / Descript — auto-transcribe your recorded interview. Always ask consent and permission first. Saves hours. Always verify accuracy.
🗂️ Desk Research
Perplexity / NotebookLM / Claude — synthesise background reading on your interviewee's domain before you go in. Acknowledge and document your usage in your Journal.
🧩 Thematic Analysis
Miro AI / Atlas.ti / ChatGPT — cluster quotes into themes. Use as a starting point, then apply YOUR judgement. Acknowledge and document all your usage in your Journal and Citation.
✍️ Writing Support
Grammarly / Claude — structure and clarity. But the insight and reflection must be genuinely yours. Acknowledge and document your usage in your Journal and Citation.

Three-Role Interview
Simulation
🎤 Interviewer
Ask questions about something the interviewee knows deeply — a hobby, TV show, skill, sport, anything. Stay curious. Probe. Follow tangents.
💬 Interviewee
Pick your own topic — something you're genuinely passionate about. Be a real person, not a robot. You can be a bit reluctant. Make the interviewer work a little.
👁️ Observer
Take detailed notes. Watch body language. Note what worked, what felt awkward, when the energy shifted. You'll give 2 mins of feedback after.
①
Round 1: 15 min interview → 5 min debrief. Switch roles.
②
Repeat x3 so everyone plays every role. Total: 3 × 20 min.
③
Final share-back: 1 minute per group — what was your biggest revelation?

Interview Technique
The question
spectrum
🔽
Small / Warm-Up: "How long have you been doing this?" — Gets them talking, builds rapport.
↕
Descriptive: "Walk me through a typical day." — Opens narrative thinking.
↑
Interpretive: "Why do you think that happens?" — Gets beneath the surface.
🔼
Projective: "If you could change one thing about your field, what would it be?" — Reveals values and vision.
🚫 Questions to Avoid
✗
Leading: "So you must love working with clients, right?"
✗
Double-barrelled: "Do you enjoy your work and is it stressful?"
✗
Yes/No: "Do you like your job?" (unless as a warm-up)
✗
Jargon-heavy: "How do you approach iterative ideation cycles?"
Google's UXR team trains researchers in "ladder up / ladder down" — move between concrete and abstract to find the real insight layer.

📝 Activity 3 · 40 min
Prep for your interview
Use your process journal. This is working time — not passive listening.
Step 1 · Desk Review
Research the domain of your potential interviewee. Journal articles, podcasts, YouTube, Reddit. You want to arrive as an informed beginner — not clueless, but genuinely curious. Use Perplexity or NotebookLM to build a knowledge base fast.
Step 2 · Find Your Person
Pick someone outside your usual circle. Not a classmate, not a family member if possible. An expert in something completely foreign to you — a sushi chef, a cardiac surgeon, a competitive Scrabble player, a volcanologist. The stranger the better.
Step 3 · Interview Protocol
Write your guide: goal of interview, duration (~1–2 hrs), 4–6 themes with 3–5 questions each, consent statement, how you'll record (ask permission!), how you'll introduce yourself and explain the project.
Step 4 · Plan the Logistics
In-person is strongly preferred. Zoom loses nuance — body language, the objects around them, the pause before the hard answer. Book a time. Have a backup. Prepare your recording setup.

Interview Protocol
Your interview
blueprint
A good protocol is not a script — it's a map. You may not visit every destination, but knowing the territory means you're never lost.
"The best interviewers come over-prepared so they can be completely present."
— Adapted from IDEO HCD Toolkit
Semi-structured preferred for A1
1–2 hrs duration
📄 Protocol Template
①
Header: Date, location, interviewee role/expertise, interviewer (you)
②
Goal statement: 1 sentence — what do you want to understand?
③
Intro script: Who you are, what this is for, consent to record
④
Theme 1–5: Each theme has 3–5 questions, from small to large
⑤
Closing: "Is there anything you'd like to add or ask me?"
⑥
Thank you + next steps: Will you share findings? Follow-up?
